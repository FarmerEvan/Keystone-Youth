Out of Egypt

The Groan, The Rescue, and The Great "I AM" (Exodus 1-3)

🙏 Opening Prayer

"Heavenly Father, as we open Your Word today, we ask for the guidance and wisdom of Your Holy Spirit. Give us eyes to see the historical truths of Exodus and hearts to understand what they mean for us today. Help us not just to learn facts, but to encounter You, the Great 'I AM'. Guide our conversation so that every voice is heard, and show us how to apply this mighty story of rescue to our own daily lives. In Jesus' name, Amen."

🎯 Key Takeaways for Leaders

  • God Hears the Groans: 400 years of silence does not mean God is absent. He sees oppression, remembers His covenant, and acts in His perfect timing.
  • Divine vs. Human Deliverance: Moses tried to deliver an Israelite in his own strength (murdering the Egyptian) and failed. True deliverance requires God's power.
  • The Nature of God: The name "I AM WHO I AM" reveals a God who is eternal, self-existent, unchanging, and deeply personal.

📚 Context & Study

Historical & Literary Context

There is a roughly 400-year gap between the end of Genesis (Joseph saving his family) and the beginning of Exodus. The Hebrews grew from a family of 70 into a massive nation. The new Pharaoh felt threatened by their numbers and enslaved them. This book transitions Israel from a wandering family into a structured, covenantal nation.

Word Study: "I AM" (Yahweh)

In Exodus 3:14, God reveals His name: "I AM WHO I AM" (Hebrew: Ehyeh asher ehyeh). This is related to the divine name YHWH (Yahweh). It comes from the Hebrew verb "to be." It means God is the uncreated Creator—He depends on nothing for His existence. Translation note: When the Old Testament was translated into Greek (the Septuagint), this was translated as ego eimi—the exact phrase Jesus uses in John 8:58 ("Before Abraham was, I AM"), claiming to be the God of the burning bush.

🔍 Observation & Interpretation

Observation: What does the text say?

  • Pharaoh issues an edict to kill all Hebrew baby boys, but the midwives (Shiphrah and Puah) fear God and refuse.
  • Moses is saved in a waterproofed papyrus basket (an ark) and adopted by Pharaoh's daughter.
  • Moses kills an Egyptian beating a Hebrew, is discovered, and flees to Midian for 40 years.
  • God speaks to Moses from a burning bush that is not consumed, calling him to go to Pharaoh and demand Israel's release.

Interpretation: What does it mean?

  • The Weak Defeat the Strong: God uses faithful women (midwives, Moses' mother, his sister, and even Pharaoh's daughter) to outsmart the most powerful king on earth. God subverts human power structures.
  • Grace in the Call: Moses protests five times against God's call (Who am I? What is your name? What if they don't believe me? I don't speak well. Send someone else). God doesn't list Moses' good qualities; He simply promises, "I will be with you." Our calling is based on God's presence, not our abilities.

🗣️ Interactive Discussion

Use these open-ended questions to foster dialogue. Wait for answers, and explicitly encourage quieter students to share their thoughts.

  1. When Moses murdered the Egyptian, he looked "in all directions" to see if anyone was watching, but he forgot to look UP. Have you ever tried to hide a sin? What happens when we try to take matters into our own hands?
  2. Read Exodus 2:23-25. The Israelites "groaned" under slavery. Why is it comforting to know that God hears our groans even before we have the perfect words to pray?
  3. When God called Moses from the burning bush, Moses made a ton of excuses. If God asked you to do something big for Him right now, what excuse would you be most likely to use?

⚖️ Discussion Debate: The Justified Murder?

Divide the room or table into two groups. Assign them a side to argue based on Exodus 2:11-12. Give them 3 minutes to prepare their arguments, then moderate a friendly debate.

Side A: Moses Was Justified

  • The Egyptian was brutally beating an enslaved, innocent Hebrew.
  • Moses was demonstrating a heart for justice and acting as a protector.
  • Sometimes extreme oppression requires extreme action to save a life.

Side B: Moses Was Wrong

  • Murder is against God's character. Moses acted in secret, proving he knew it was wrong.
  • He acted in his own flesh/anger rather than waiting for God's instruction.
  • His actions didn't actually help his people; it just made him a fugitive for 40 years.

Leader Conclusion: While Moses had a God-given desire for justice, he used worldly, sinful methods to achieve it. God had to send him to the desert for 40 years to strip away his reliance on his own strength before he was ready to lead.

🚀 Application & Action

Connect to Daily Life: The story of the Exodus is the ultimate picture of salvation. Just as Israel was enslaved in Egypt, Jesus teaches that we are enslaved to sin. Just as God sent a rescuer (Moses) to lead them out, God sent Jesus (The Great I AM) to rescue us.

Action Steps for the Week:

  • Identify Your "Egypt": What is an area of your life right now where you feel stuck, trapped, or enslaved (e.g., a bad habit, a toxic friendship, fear, anxiety)? Write it down in a journal.
  • The "I AM" Challenge: When Moses said "I can't," God said, "I AM." Every time you doubt yourself this week or feel afraid, replace your "I can't" with God's promise from Exodus 3:12: "I will be with you."
